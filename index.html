Song + Music Video

For Röya — An AI-Assisted Music Video

A personal music video created as a love letter for my wife, Röya, combining songwriting, visual storytelling, and AI-assisted production.

Watch on YouTube
Creative thought process

This music video was a deeply personal project I created for my wife, Röya. I began by writing, in my own plain words, everything I felt about her and about our relationship, especially memories from our early dating days. Once I had the emotional core of the piece, I worked with Gemini to help refine those ideas into lyrics with stronger rhyme, rhythm, and structure while still preserving my voice and the meaning behind the words.

From there, I used Suno to turn the lyrics into a finished song, shaping elements like the melody, pacing, and overall musical feel. Once the song was complete, my attention shifted to how I wanted to represent it visually. I’ve always loved pre-industrial storytelling, especially the emotional atmosphere of castle-era worlds, where a warrior could be away from home for a long time and be sustained by the thought of a loved one waiting for him. That became the emotional and visual foundation of the video. I also wanted there to be some ambiguity — whether the warrior is truly reunited with his lover, or whether those moments are partly unfolding in his mind.

One of the biggest challenges was consistency. At the time, I was working with Flow using VEO 3, which was limited to generating 8-second clips, while the full music video runs almost four minutes. To solve that, I first storyboarded the entire piece in Opus 4.5, breaking it into roughly 8-second segments. For each segment, I generated a starting frame and an ending frame using detailed descriptions and a JSON-style guide, which helped maintain consistency through ChatGPT image generation. I intentionally chose a black-and-white illustrated style rather than realism, because illustration is much more forgiving of the visual quirks that often appear in AI image and video generation.

After building the sequence, I generated the clips in Flow, stitched them together in post-production, and extended some moments by slowing down the footage. The entire project took about 12 hours, and I’m very proud of the result. In the future, I’d love to revisit this process with a tool like Runway to achieve even stronger consistency and fidelity with less manual effort.

Where Are You, Youth? / Молодость, ты где?

A Russian-language AI-assisted song and music video released under the stage name Адам Бакинский. For English-speaking listeners: Молодость, ты где? translates to Where Are You, Youth?, with the public-facing credit as Adam Rakhamim.

Watch on YouTube
Creative thought process

This project was both a musical challenge and a visual storytelling challenge. The song speaks about the nostalgia we attach to youth: the parts that are real, the parts that are romanticized, and the emotional tension between who we were and who we become.

The songwriting process was similar to some of my other songs. I started by writing my own piece, then used AI to help structure it so it could work musically. What makes this project especially interesting is the video. All of the images in the video are AI-generated, and each pair of images is designed to show a contrast between the present and the remembered past.

For example, one image shows a desolate, broken-down playground with rusted equipment and nothing around it. Its corresponding image shows the same playground during youth, filled with children joyfully playing. Another image shows a woman behind a desk doing accounting work, visibly stressed. The matching image shows that same woman as a young lady, with her desk covered not in accounting papers, but in art papers, paint, and brushes.

On the AI side, generating these images with consistent camera angles, consistent characters, and clear visual parallels was difficult. It required carefully defining the angle, the number of people in the shot, and the relationship between each present-day image and its corresponding memory.

Song

My Baku / Мой Баку

A Russian-language AI-assisted song released under the stage name Адам Бакинский. For English-speaking listeners: Мой Баку translates to My Baku, and the public-facing credit is Adam Rakhamim.

Open on YouTube
Creative thought process

This song is about Baku, the hometown I grew up in. I wrote the original words myself, which is how I usually begin this kind of creative process: by jotting down my own thoughts, memories, and feelings before bringing in any AI tools. For this song, I leaned into what I carry deep in my heart about my love for the city.

I also researched online forums to understand how other people from Baku, especially those living abroad, spoke about the city. The song became an amalgamation of the love, nostalgia, and longing that many people who grew up in Baku seem to carry with them. I shaped those feelings into a poem as best as I could, then worked with Gemini to refine the structure so the syllabic flow felt consistent and pleasant to the ear.

From there, I took the lyrics into Suno. After quite a few generations and attempts, I found a chanson-inspired style that felt right for the nostalgic tone of the words. I then used ChatGPT to create a cover image and put the final visual together in Clipchamp, including details like the music bars.

As of May 2026, this song has received over 20,000 views. I’ve also had people message me to say that the song captured their feelings about Baku, which is one of the most meaningful responses I could have hoped for.

I Look

An English-language AI-assisted song released as “I Look | Adam Rakhamim,” built around searching, direction, longing, and self-reflection.

Open on YouTube
Creative thought process

This song was another way for me to express what I was feeling during a particular week. Sometimes I hit moments where I do not know exactly which direction my life is heading, and my first instinct is to look outward for an answer. I might look to other people, a book, a motivational video, or some outside source of clarity.

The song captures that emotional movement: searching outside myself for help, then realizing that the answer also requires looking inward. It is about the moment when external inspiration is useful, but not enough by itself. At some point, I have to lean in and confront what is happening inside me.

The process was interesting because it began as a monologue that I wrote into ChatGPT. I used ChatGPT to help structure that monologue into lyrics, then brought the lyrics into Suno to turn them into a finished song.

Song

Bibble Bobble Bubblegum

A playful AI-assisted song released as “Adam R - Bibble Bobble Bubblegum,” filled with colorful characters, tongue-twisting rhymes, and bubbly energy.

Open on YouTube
Creative thought process

This was my first attempt at making music with AI, and it was both fun and surprisingly challenging. My goal was simple: make a song that my kids would actually enjoy. Based on the number of times we played it in the car, I would call it a success.

I focused on alliteration, rhythm, and finding a loop that could get stuck in someone’s head. The song tells a playful story about a character going on an adventure, but the fast, joyful beat and the repeated “Bibble Bobble” sound are what make it memorable.

I created the image for the song using one of ChatGPT’s earlier image generation models. Looking back, I remember how much harder it was to create usable images compared to today. At that time, AI-generated characters often had missing limbs, strange proportions, or awkward details.

I leaned into an illustrated bubblegum-character style because it was more forgiving. If a bubblegum character has long legs, a big head, or odd proportions, it can still feel intentional and playful rather than broken. That made the visual style a good match for the silly, energetic spirit of the song.

Publication

Animals and Seasons

My first attempt at creating an illustrated children’s book using AI, made in 2022 with OpenAI’s DALL·E 2 image model.

View on Amazon
Creative thought process

Animals and Seasons was my first attempt to create an illustrated book using AI. Back in 2022, I was working with OpenAI’s DALL·E 2 image model, which by today’s standards feels very rudimentary. Details like eyes, ears, and legs were often way off, and one of the biggest challenges was that the model could not reliably create consistent characters from one generation to the next, even with detailed descriptions.

At the time, I asked myself: what format would be most forgiving if I wanted to create a completed piece of work with the AI tools available then? I was reading stories to my kids, and I thought a children’s book could work well because it could include many different characters doing different things while still feeling cohesive. My son was around two years old, so I thought about what he would enjoy hearing and what kinds of words he was learning. Animals and seasons felt like a natural fit.

I wrote the whole book myself because, at the time, ChatGPT was not yet especially useful for helping me write the poem. The opening lines are simple: “Here’s a chicken in the summer and a kitten in the fall. There’s a bear during the winter and in the spring a giraffe so tall.” Those lines allowed me to create four separate AI-generated images and bring them together into one spread.

I only had to make minor corrections in a few places. For example, the bear’s eyes were slightly too far apart and looked unnatural, so I used a little Photoshop to adjust them. For the kitten image, some of the falling leaves did not always look like leaves, so I corrected those details as well. For 2022, this felt like an amazing accomplishment: AI-generated illustrations assembled into a real children’s book. I was proud of it, my kids loved it, and the project gave me a chance to push what AI could do at that moment in time.

Publication

How to Throw the Best Tea Party in the World

A 2025 children’s book project created to test how far AI-assisted image generation and storytelling workflows had advanced since my first AI book in 2022.

View on Amazon
Creative thought process

How to Throw the Best Tea Party in the World was published in 2025, three years after Animals and Seasons. This book was my attempt to see, in practice, how much easier it had become to create a children’s book with AI after several years of progress in image-generation models.

With Animals and Seasons, I had to work around the limitations of early AI illustration tools. By 2025, I wanted to test whether I could prompt an AI model to help bring together a more complete book experience, including a more cohesive visual world. There was still a fair amount of inconsistency, and I still had to guide the storyline and creative direction carefully, but the model was able to produce a much higher degree of consistency than what was possible three years earlier.

For me, this second book was less about proving that AI could replace the creative process and more about measuring how far the tools had come. I wanted to compare the experience of building a children’s book in 2025 against the experience of building one in 2022. The difference was significant.

I plan to make another children’s book, maybe this year or early next year, and I expect that process to feel dramatically different again. The image and video generation models released over the last year have improved so quickly that each new project becomes a snapshot of what the technology could do at that specific moment.
